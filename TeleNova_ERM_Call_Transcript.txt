ERM ENGAGEMENT CALL TRANSCRIPT
================================
Client: TeleNova Communications (Fictional)
Interviewee: Rajiv Menon, Chief Risk Officer
Interviewer: Priya Sharma, Partner - Risk Advisory
Duration: ~55 minutes
Date: April 2026

================================
TRANSCRIPT
================================

Priya: Rajiv, thanks for making the time. You've already gone through the questionnaire and the impact table with your team so we won't go back over any of that. The idea today is really just a conversation - I want to hear from you directly, in your own words. Let's start broad.

---
Q1 - Walk us through the risks you would put in front of your board today.
---

Priya: If you were sitting in front of your board tomorrow morning, what's the risk list you'd walk them through?

Rajiv: Sure. So the first thing I'd lead with is 5G monetization - we've made enormous capital commitments to the rollout and frankly the revenue model hasn't materialized the way we projected. Enterprise use cases are slower to develop than anyone expected and the consumer side is still largely indifferent to paying a premium for 5G. So there's a real question of whether we recover that investment on the timeline we told the board.

Then I'd talk about cyber. It's not new but it's getting worse. We're seeing a significant increase in the volume and sophistication of attacks on our infrastructure - not just the usual credential-based stuff but targeted attacks on network infrastructure itself. Given the geopolitical environment, we think some of this is state-adjacent. That concerns me deeply.

Third would be regulation. We're operating across multiple jurisdictions and the regulatory environment is moving fast - data privacy, spectrum allocation, 5G-specific regulations, the European data rules. Keeping pace with all of that is becoming a serious resource and compliance challenge.

Then I'd flag competition - the OTT players are not going away. WhatsApp, Teams, all of them are eating into our traditional voice and messaging revenue. And now we're starting to see some of them explore direct infrastructure partnerships which is a different kind of threat.

Energy costs are a big one - our data centres and network infrastructure have significant energy exposure and with prices where they are, it's directly impacting our cost base. We haven't cracked the sustainability angle either which is increasingly a board conversation.

And then talent - we're struggling to hire the right people for the technology transformation we're going through. The skills we need for cloud, for cybersecurity, for AI - these people have options and we're not always winning.

Priya: That's a solid starting list. Any others before we go deeper?

Rajiv: I'd add infrastructure resilience. We had two significant outages last year - one was weather-related, one was a software failure - and both times it exposed how thin our redundancy actually is. And I'd probably mention supplier dependency. We are heavily concentrated in a small number of vendors for critical network components and that's a risk we haven't fully addressed.

---
Q2 - Are there risks on your radar that haven't made it onto your formal register yet?
---

Priya: What's on your personal radar that hasn't been formally documented yet - things you're watching but haven't gone through the governance process?

Rajiv: Two things. First is AI - specifically the risks that come with deploying AI in our customer-facing and back-office operations. We're starting to use it aggressively but the governance framework isn't there yet. Model errors, bias in decision-making, customer data exposure - these are real and I don't think we've properly assessed them.

Second is what I'd call the free-to-use disruption. There are business models emerging - particularly in developing markets but also starting to appear here - where connectivity is bundled or subsidized by platforms. If that model scales, it fundamentally undermines how we price. It's not on the register because it sounds speculative but I think it deserves serious attention.

---
Q3 - Which risks do you consider permanent fixtures for a telecom - inherent to the business regardless of external environment?
---

Priya: Thinking specifically about your industry - what risks do you consider inherent? Things that will always be there simply because of what you do?

Rajiv: Cyber is inherent - full stop. We are critical national infrastructure. We will always be a target. That never goes away regardless of how good our defences are.

Data privacy is the same - we hold enormous amounts of customer data, location data, communication records. The obligation and the risk is permanent.

Network outages - some level of infrastructure failure is inherent. You can reduce it but you can't eliminate it.

Regulatory compliance - the nature of our licensing and the way governments treat telecoms means we will always be heavily regulated. That's not going away.

And I'd say spectrum - competition for spectrum is structural to this industry. It's always going to be a constraint and a cost.

---
Q4 - Which of your risks are most sensitive to external events?
---

Priya: Flip side of that - which risks on your list are the ones that are manageable today but could escalate very quickly if something changes externally?

Rajiv: Energy costs immediately. If there's another geopolitical shock that drives energy prices up further, our cost base takes a significant hit and our sustainability commitments become even harder to meet. That's directly tied to external events.

Geopolitical tension and infrastructure attacks - we've seen in Ukraine and elsewhere that telecoms infrastructure becomes a target in conflict situations. A serious escalation in European tensions specifically worries me.

Currency - we have operations and financing in multiple currencies and market volatility hits us on both sides.

Regulation is event-driven in a different way - a single court ruling or a new government coming in with a different stance on data or spectrum can reshape our compliance obligations overnight.

And honestly the OTT competition risk is event-driven as well - if one of the big platforms announces a major infrastructure play or a partnership with a rival operator, the competitive landscape changes immediately.

---
Q5 - Have you had any significant risk events or close calls in the last two to three years?
---

Priya: You mentioned the two outages. Can you walk me through those in a bit more detail - and any other events?

Rajiv: The weather event was a tropical storm that took out three of our tower clusters in the coastal region. We had SLA obligations with enterprise clients that we breached. The financial impact was manageable - the reputational damage with those clients was harder to recover from. Two of them reduced their contract scope at renewal.

The software outage was more embarrassing honestly. A routine update to our network management platform caused a cascading failure that affected about 18% of our subscriber base for six hours. The root cause was an untested dependency in a legacy system that nobody had documented properly. That one exposed a real gap in our change management and legacy infrastructure risk.

We also had a data incident - not a full breach but an unauthorized access event involving a third-party vendor who had excessive system privileges. We contained it but it triggered a regulatory inquiry that took eight months to close.

---
Q6 - Are there any strategic moves currently underway that are bringing new risks into the business?
---

Priya: Any major strategic bets being made right now that you think are introducing new risk?

Rajiv: Two big ones. We're in the middle of a large-scale cloud transformation - moving core operations and customer systems off legacy infrastructure onto cloud. The complexity and the timeline risk are significant. We're already six months behind the original plan and every delay has cost implications.

We're also exploring an infrastructure sharing arrangement with one of our competitors - passive and active sharing for some network assets. On paper it makes financial sense but the governance, the operational dependencies, and the regulatory approval process are all creating new risk vectors we're still working through.

---
Q7 - What regulatory or policy changes are you tracking most closely right now?
---

Priya: Which specific regulatory developments are you most concerned about?

Rajiv: The European data regulations - particularly how they're being interpreted and enforced across different member states. GDPR has been around long enough but the enforcement is becoming more aggressive and the fines are material.

5G-specific regulations - there's active debate in several of our markets about health safety standards for 5G, specifically EMF exposure limits. If those standards tighten it affects our rollout plans and potentially creates product liability exposure.

Carbon disclosure requirements - we're now being required to make detailed climate performance disclosures and frankly our data quality isn't where it needs to be to do that confidently. That's a compliance risk in the near term.

And spectrum policy - there are auctions coming up in two of our key markets and the regulatory framing of those auctions will significantly affect our capital planning.

---
Q8 - Where is your business most exposed to financial and market risks?
---

Priya: On the financial and market side - where are you most exposed?

Rajiv: Currency is real for us - we generate revenue in local currencies but a significant portion of our capital expenditure and debt is denominated in dollars and euros. Any sustained move against us is painful.

The continuous capital requirement is the deeper issue. This is a capital-intensive industry and the 5G investment cycle on top of the fibre rollout means we are in a sustained period of high capital expenditure. If credit conditions tighten or our credit rating comes under pressure, that creates a financing risk.

Energy price volatility directly affects our operating cost and therefore our EBITDA margin. It's the single biggest variable cost exposure we have right now.

---
Q9 - How dependent is your business on external parties where a disruption could have an enterprise-level impact?
---

Priya: Supplier and third-party dependency - how concentrated are you?

Rajiv: Very concentrated in places we probably shouldn't be. For our core network equipment we are essentially dependent on two vendors. That's it. If either of them has supply chain issues - and we've seen that happen - our rollout timelines are immediately affected. The geopolitical dimension makes this worse because one of those vendors has exposure to trade restriction risk.

On the software side, our network management platform comes from a single vendor and as I mentioned the update failure last year showed us how fragile that dependency is.

We also have significant reliance on third-party managed service providers for parts of our operations and the alignment on standards and incident response has been inconsistent.

---
Q10 - What competitive or market disruptions do you believe pose the biggest threat to your business model?
---

Priya: Where do you see the biggest competitive disruption threats?

Rajiv: OTT players collaborating directly with operators or building their own infrastructure - that's the scenario that worries me most. If a major platform decides to buy spectrum or partner with a hyperscaler to deliver connectivity directly, our role in the value chain shrinks significantly.

The content war is relevant too - we've tried to be a content aggregator and it hasn't worked as well as we hoped. The direct-to-consumer move by content producers is squeezing the bundled model we relied on.

And the hyperscalers - AWS, Azure, Google - are moving deeper into network infrastructure. Edge computing, private 5G networks - they're building capability that overlaps with ours. That's not a five-year threat anymore, it's a two-year threat.

---
Q11 - At an enterprise level, how significant is technology risk - cyber, digital disruption, or obsolescence?
---

Priya: Technology risk more broadly - how does it sit for you?

Rajiv: Cyber is our number one technology risk, no question. And within cyber I want to separate two things - the external attack risk which is real and growing, and the insider and third-party access risk which I think is underweighted in most assessments. The vendor access incident I mentioned earlier was the latter.

Legacy infrastructure is a significant risk that doesn't get enough attention. We have systems running that are fifteen, twenty years old. They weren't designed for the current threat environment and they can't be easily replaced without disrupting operations. The cloud migration is partly about addressing this but it's a multi-year journey.

IoT is an emerging technology risk for us. We're building out IoT solutions for enterprise clients and the security architecture for that is genuinely complex. We have pockets of expertise but not consistent capability across the organization.

---
Q12 - If your most significant risk materialized, which other risks would be pulled in alongside it?
---

Priya: Let's talk about how your risks connect. If your biggest risk - let's take the cyber scenario - actually materialized, what else gets pulled in?

Rajiv: A serious cyber event - particularly one targeting network infrastructure - immediately triggers network outage which triggers SLA breaches which triggers customer trust and reputational damage. Simultaneously you have regulatory notification obligations under data protection rules which triggers regulatory scrutiny. If customer data is exposed that's a separate litigation and penalty risk. And in today's environment if it looks like a state-adjacent attack, there are geopolitical and government relations dimensions as well. A single serious cyber event is simultaneously a network risk, a data risk, a regulatory risk, a reputational risk, and potentially a national security conversation. They're not separate.

Priya: What about on the financial side - if the 5G monetization issue persists?

Rajiv: Then capital pressure builds. We start having to make choices about which rollouts to delay - which affects our spectrum commitments and regulatory obligations. Delayed rollout affects enterprise customer acquisition which affects revenue further. And if credit conditions are already tight we may face a refinancing risk. It cascades from a commercial problem into a financial and regulatory problem quite quickly.

---
Q13 - Have you seen a risk that appeared contained but ended up triggering a much larger chain of consequences?
---

Priya: Can you give me a real example of a risk that started small but cascaded?

Rajiv: The software outage I described is actually a good example. It started as what looked like a routine software patch issue - a technical problem, contained to a platform team. Six hours later it's 18% of subscribers without service, the CEO is on calls with major enterprise clients, we're issuing public communications, the regulator is asking questions, and the board wants a full post-incident report. What began as a change management failure became a reputational event, a customer retention risk, and a regulatory touchpoint within one day.

---
Q14 - What risks are you most concerned about 18 to 24 months from now that aren't formally on your register today?
---

Priya: Looking forward - what's not on your register today that you think should be?

Rajiv: AI governance is the most important one. We are deploying AI faster than we are governing it. The risk of a significant AI-related failure - whether that's a customer-facing decision error, a data exposure, or a regulatory action - is growing every month.

The free-to-use connectivity model I mentioned - I want to formally assess that because if it scales it's an existential business model risk not just a competitive nuisance.

And electronic waste - we haven't formally assessed the regulatory and reputational risk around the hardware disposal cycle as we accelerate technology upgrades. That's coming whether we're ready or not.

---
Q15 - Which risks do you feel your organization is most underprepared for right now?
---

Priya: Last question - honest assessment. Where are you most underprepared?

Rajiv: Three areas. Legacy infrastructure risk - we talk about it but we haven't properly quantified what a serious failure event looks like financially and operationally. The cloud migration is supposed to solve it but we're behind schedule and the legacy systems are aging in the meantime.

Third-party and supply chain risk - we have concentration in critical vendors that we haven't adequately mitigated and the geopolitical dimension makes it more urgent than our current posture reflects.

And AI governance - it's moving fast, we don't have the framework, and we're accumulating risk every quarter we don't address it.

Priya: Rajiv, this has been extremely useful. We'll come back to you with the mapped risk universe and the initial scoring within the next few days. Thank you.

Rajiv: Thank you, Priya. Looking forward to seeing what comes out of it.

END OF TRANSCRIPT
