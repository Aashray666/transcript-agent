
### Sample ERM Engagement Call Transcript
**Client:** VelocityAuto Group (fictional)
**Interviewee:** Sanjay Kulkarni, Chief Risk Officer
**Interviewer:** Priya Sharma, Partner – Risk Advisory
**Duration:** ~65 minutes
**Sector:** Automotive Manufacturing

***

**Priya:** Sanjay, thanks for making the time. You've already gone through the questionnaire and the impact table with your team so we won't revisit those today. This is purely a conversation — I want to hear from you directly. Let's get straight into it.

***

**Q1. "What are your top risks right now? Walk us through the list."**

**Priya:** If you were walking your board through your top risks today, what's the list?

**Sanjay:** Sure. Let me go through them as I'd present them.

First is EV transition risk. We've committed heavily to electrification — new platforms, battery supply agreements, retooling three plants — and the market isn't moving at the pace we planned for. Consumer adoption in our core markets has slowed significantly. There's a real tension between the capital we've already deployed and the revenue timeline we projected. We're now having internal conversations about whether to slow the transition roadmap which creates its own set of risks with regulators and investors.

Second is tariffs and trade policy. The US tariff environment post-2025 has been extremely disruptive. We have significant North American exposure — both in terms of components we import and vehicles we export. The 25% tariffs on auto imports that came into effect have directly hit our cost structure. And the uncertainty is almost as damaging as the tariffs themselves because we can't plan supply chain decisions confidently.

Third is supply chain — specifically battery raw materials. We are dependent on lithium, cobalt, and nickel sourced heavily from a small number of geographies. China controls a significant portion of the processing capacity for these materials and that concentration is a strategic vulnerability we haven't fully resolved.

Fourth is software and technology risk. Modern vehicles are essentially software platforms now. Our capability to develop, update, and secure vehicle software is lagging behind pure-play EV competitors and that gap is becoming a competitive risk. We also have cybersecurity exposure in connected vehicles that we're actively working on but it's not where it needs to be.

Fifth would be dealer network disruption. The shift to direct-to-consumer models by some competitors — particularly EV-native brands — is creating pressure on our traditional dealer model. We've had some very difficult conversations with dealer groups about the future of the relationship.

Sixth is workforce transformation. We're shutting down or retooling internal combustion engine production lines and the workforce implications are significant — retraining, redundancies, union negotiations. In Germany and the UK particularly this is a live and sensitive issue.

And seventh I'd add regulatory compliance — specifically emissions regulations. Euro 7 standards, the US EPA rules, and equivalent standards in other markets are all tightening. Our ICE vehicle lineup has a defined compliance window and managing that transition without stranding assets is genuinely complex.

**Priya:** That's a strong opening list. Anything else before we go deeper?

**Sanjay:** I'd add geopolitical risk around our China operations. We have a significant joint venture in China — it's been one of our most profitable operations historically — but the market there has deteriorated sharply. Local EV brands, particularly BYD and others, have taken enormous market share. And the geopolitical dimension between China and Western markets adds uncertainty to the long-term viability of that JV structure.

***

**Q2. "Are there any risks you're tracking that aren't on your risk register yet?"**

**Priya:** What's on your personal radar that isn't formally documented yet?

**Sanjay:** Two things. The first is AI liability in vehicles. We're integrating AI-based driver assistance systems and the legal and liability framework for when those systems fail is genuinely unclear. There's no settled case law, there's no consistent regulatory standard across jurisdictions. We're accumulating liability exposure we can't fully quantify yet.

The second is what I'd call the stranded asset risk on ICE. As emissions regulations tighten and consumer demand shifts, we have manufacturing plants, tooling, and supply agreements built around internal combustion engines that may have a shorter useful life than their book value implies. We haven't done a formal impairment assessment on that scenario yet and I think we should.

***

**Q3. "Which risks do you think will always exist in your industry — no matter what the business does?"**

**Priya:** What risks are simply permanent fixtures in automotive regardless of what the business does?

**Sanjay:** Product safety and recall risk — that will never go away. The consequences of a safety failure — reputational, financial, regulatory — are always present.

Cyclicality is inherent. Automotive demand is fundamentally tied to economic conditions, consumer confidence, and credit availability. That has always been true.

Labour and industrial relations — we operate with large, heavily unionised workforces in multiple countries. That dynamic is permanent.

Regulatory compliance — we will always be one of the most regulated industries in the world, whether it's safety standards, emissions, or increasingly software and data regulations.

And raw material dependency — we will always be exposed to commodity price volatility. Whether it's steel and aluminium today or lithium and cobalt tomorrow, that exposure is structural.

***

**Q4. "Which of your risks could escalate quickly if something changed externally?"**

**Priya:** Which risks are sitting at a manageable level today but could spike very quickly if something changed outside the business?

**Sanjay:** Tariffs are the obvious one. We've already seen one escalation cycle and a second round — either broader US tariffs or retaliatory measures from the EU or China — would hit us hard and fast. We have almost no short-term flexibility in our supply chain to absorb another shock.

Battery raw material prices — specifically lithium and cobalt — are volatile and tied to mining output, Chinese processing capacity, and demand from multiple industries competing for the same supply. A supply shock there would be immediate and severe.

The China JV situation could deteriorate rapidly if US-China trade relations escalate further. We have significant capital tied up in that structure and an exit or forced restructuring would be very costly.

And currency — we manufacture in euros and sell globally. A sharp dollar strengthening or yuan movement hits our margins directly.

***

**Q5. "Have you had any major risk events or close calls in the last two to three years? What happened?"**

**Priya:** Walk me through any significant risk events or near-misses in the last two to three years.

**Sanjay:** We had a significant semiconductor shortage event in 2023 — we were forced to halt production at two plants for a combined six weeks because we couldn't source specific microcontrollers. The financial hit was over 400 million euros in lost production. That single event fundamentally changed how we think about supply chain concentration.

We had a product recall in 2024 — a software defect in an ADAS feature across approximately 180,000 vehicles. No serious injuries, fortunately, but the recall cost was substantial and the reputational impact in a market where we were trying to establish our EV credentials was damaging at exactly the wrong time.

And last year we had a ransomware incident at one of our tier-one suppliers. It didn't directly breach our systems but it disrupted component supply for three weeks while they recovered. It reinforced how exposed we are through our supply chain's cyber posture, not just our own.

***

**Q6. "Are there any big strategic moves happening right now that are bringing in new risks?"**

**Priya:** Any major strategic initiatives underway that are introducing new risk into the business?

**Sanjay:** Yes — three. We're launching our first purpose-built EV platform next year. The complexity of that program — new battery architecture, new software stack, new manufacturing process — all at once is significant execution risk. We're behind schedule on the software validation and that's causing tension with the launch timeline.

We're also in the middle of a joint venture with a battery manufacturer to secure our own cell production capacity. The capital commitment is large and the technology risk — cell chemistry is still evolving — means we could be investing in a chemistry that's partially obsolete in five years.

And we're restructuring our China JV. We're trying to rebalance the ownership structure and governance to protect ourselves better but the negotiation is complex and there's a real risk of disruption to operations during the transition.

***

**Q7. "Which regulatory or policy changes are you watching most closely and why?"**

**Priya:** Which regulatory developments are keeping you up at night specifically?

**Sanjay:** Euro 7 is the most immediate. The new emissions standards coming into effect create a very tight compliance window for our remaining ICE models. The cost of compliance per vehicle is significant and we're having to decide which models to carry forward and which to discontinue.

The EU's 2035 ICE ban — there's still political uncertainty around whether it holds, whether there are carve-outs, and on what timeline. We've built our transition roadmap around it and if it shifts materially in either direction it disrupts our planning.

US CAFE standards and EPA emissions rules — the regulatory posture has shifted and we're watching that carefully in terms of what it means for our North American ICE lineup.

Data privacy regulations affecting connected vehicles are also emerging. We collect significant data from connected vehicles and the regulatory requirements around consent, storage, and cross-border data transfer are tightening across multiple jurisdictions.

And the EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive — CSRD — is imposing significant new disclosure obligations on us. Our data infrastructure isn't ready for the granularity of reporting required.

***

**Q8. "Where is the business most exposed to financial and market risks?"**

**Priya:** Financial and market risk — where are you most exposed?

**Sanjay:** Currency is our biggest structural exposure. We price many vehicles in dollars and euros but manufacture across a broader set of currency environments. Any significant shift in major currency pairs hits our reported margins directly.

Commodity prices — steel, aluminium, and increasingly battery raw materials — are a significant variable cost exposure. We have hedging programs but they don't fully cover us beyond 12-18 months.

Capital availability for the EV transition is a real concern. The investment programme is large, we're in a period of compressed margins, and if credit conditions tighten or our credit rating comes under pressure the cost of financing that programme increases meaningfully.

And warranty and recall provisions — these are financial liabilities that can crystallize quickly and at scale if a product quality issue emerges, as we experienced with the ADAS recall.

***

**Q9. "How dependent are you on key suppliers — and where would a failure have the biggest impact?"**

**Priya:** Third-party dependency — walk me through your concentration risks.

**Sanjay:** Battery cells are our most acute concentration risk right now. We're dependent on a small number of Asian cell manufacturers and the geopolitical dimension of that concentration is something we're actively trying to reduce through the JV I mentioned — but that's a three to four year journey. In the interim the concentration is real.

Semiconductors — the 2023 experience taught us we were too concentrated in specific chip families from a small number of fabs. We've done work on dual-sourcing since then but it's incomplete.

Software platforms — we're using a small number of third-party software providers for vehicle operating systems and ADAS components. If any of those suppliers had a major quality or financial event the impact on our vehicle programmes would be immediate.

And logistics — we rely on specific port infrastructure and rail connections for just-in-time delivery. The exposure to port disruptions, particularly in Europe, is something the pandemic made very visible and it hasn't fully been addressed.

***

**Q10. "For your critical vendors — how much visibility do you have into their own risk posture?"**

**Priya:** For those critical vendors — how much do you actually know about their risk management?

**Sanjay:** Honestly, it varies enormously. For our tier-one suppliers we have reasonable visibility — audit rights, financial monitoring, some business continuity requirements in contracts. But the cyber posture of our supply chain is genuinely opaque. The ransomware event at our tier-one supplier last year was the first time we really understood how little visibility we had. They hadn't disclosed the attack to us proactively — we found out because components stopped arriving.

For battery cell suppliers specifically — these are large sophisticated companies and we have commercial relationships but not deep operational visibility into their supply chains. So our lithium concentration risk is actually one level further removed — it's their suppliers we're exposed to, not just them directly.

***

**Q11. "What competitive threats pose the biggest risk to your business model over the next few years?"**

**Priya:** Where do you see the biggest competitive disruption coming from?

**Sanjay:** Chinese EV manufacturers are the most significant structural threat. BYD in particular — they're vertically integrated in a way we're not, their cost structure is fundamentally different, and they're beginning to enter European and other markets aggressively. If European tariffs on Chinese EVs hold they're contained somewhat, but that's a policy decision not a competitive solution.

Tesla's software and over-the-air update model has set a consumer expectation that we're still catching up to. Customers now expect vehicles to improve after purchase and our ability to deliver that is still limited.

Tech companies entering the automotive space — whether directly or through partnerships — remain a threat. The vehicle software stack is increasingly where value is created and captured, and technology companies have structural advantages in software development that we don't.

And the direct-to-consumer model from EV natives is disrupting dealer economics. If consumers increasingly expect to buy directly, our dealer network — which represents significant capital and relationship investment — becomes a liability rather than an asset.

***

**Q12. "How significant is technology risk for you — cyber, legacy systems, digital disruption?"**

**Priya:** Technology risk specifically — how does it sit for you?

**Sanjay:** Connected vehicle cybersecurity is our most significant technology risk right now. Modern vehicles have dozens of software-defined components, multiple wireless interfaces, and over-the-air update capabilities. Each of those is an attack surface. The consequences of a successful cyber attack on vehicle safety systems is a completely different risk profile from a traditional data breach — we're talking about physical safety.

Legacy manufacturing systems are a real issue. We have production facilities running on systems that are fifteen to twenty years old. They're not connected to modern IT infrastructure in a managed way and they can't be patched or updated easily. That's an operational and cyber risk simultaneously.

And our internal software development capability — we're trying to build it but we're competing with Google, Amazon, and Apple for the same talent. The gap between where we need to be on software and where we are today is a genuine strategic risk.

***

**Q13. "If your biggest risk materialized tomorrow, which other risks would get triggered alongside it?"**

**Priya:** Let's talk about how these risks connect. If your 5G tariff shock materialized fully — say a second wave of US tariffs plus Chinese retaliation — what gets pulled in?

**Sanjay:** Immediately — cost of goods sold goes up significantly, margin compression hits, which puts pressure on the EV investment programme. If we have to slow the EV programme, we fall further behind on regulatory compliance timelines which creates a regulatory risk. Investor confidence drops which affects our cost of capital. And if we're seen to be retreating on electrification, we have a reputational risk with a very vocal ESG-focused investor base. One trade policy shock cascades into a financial risk, a regulatory risk, a strategic risk, and a reputational risk simultaneously.

**Priya:** What about the supply chain scenario — a battery raw material shock?

**Sanjay:** Battery supply disruption immediately threatens our EV production programme — vehicles can't be built without cells. That's a revenue and volume risk. It also potentially breaches delivery commitments to fleet customers where we have contracts with penalty clauses. If the disruption is prolonged, our JV partner's position comes under financial stress which creates a counterparty risk. And competitors who have secured better supply deals gain market share during the disruption window which is a long-tail competitive risk.

***

**Q14. "Have you seen a risk that started small but pulled in a much bigger set of consequences?"**

**Priya:** Give me a real example of a cascade you've actually lived through.

**Sanjay:** The ADAS software recall in 2024 is the clearest example. It started as a software validation issue flagged by an internal quality team — a functional edge case in the lane-keeping system. At that point it was an engineering problem. But once we determined the scale of affected vehicles and the regulatory notification obligation was triggered, it became simultaneously a regulatory event, a communications crisis, a legal liability issue, and a customer trust problem. The CEO was personally involved within 48 hours. The board was briefed. The financial provision was significant. What started as a software bug became an enterprise-level event within two weeks.

***

**Q15. "Looking back at your last formal risk assessment — were there risks that materialized that weren't on your register?"**

**Priya:** When you look back at your last formal risk assessment — were there things that actually happened that weren't on the register?

**Sanjay:** Yes, honestly. The speed and scale of Chinese EV penetration in the European market was not something we had formally assessed at the level it deserved twelve months ago. We had it as a distant strategic watch item, not an active risk. That was an error of judgment — we were anchored to historical market entry patterns from Chinese manufacturers which had been slow. BYD and others broke that pattern and we were caught reacting rather than prepared.

The regulatory uncertainty around the 2035 ICE ban also surprised us — we had treated it as settled policy and built plans around it. The political reopening of that debate in Europe created genuine uncertainty in our investment planning that we hadn't stress-tested for.

***

**Q16. "For the risks you've mentioned, where do you have controls in place — and where are they weak or missing?"**

**Priya:** For the risks you've described, where do you actually have controls in place and where are the gaps?

**Sanjay:** Product safety — strong controls. We have mature processes, regulatory compliance frameworks, and a well-resourced quality function. That's our strongest area.

Supply chain concentration — partially controlled. We have dual-sourcing requirements in policy but execution is incomplete, particularly for semiconductors and battery materials. The policy exists; the actual supply diversification doesn't fully yet.

Cyber — controls exist but they're not uniform. Our corporate IT environment is well managed. Our manufacturing OT environment and our connected vehicle infrastructure are significantly less mature.

Currency and commodity hedging — we have formal treasury programmes with board-approved parameters. Reasonable coverage within those parameters.

EV transition execution — limited formal risk controls. This is a programme management risk more than anything and our programme governance for the EV platform launch is under-resourced relative to the complexity.

China JV — governance controls are being strengthened as part of the restructuring but we're in the middle of that process. Right now it's a gap.

***

**Q17. "Where a control exists, has it actually been tested — or is it more on paper?"**

**Priya:** For the controls you mentioned — which ones have actually been tested versus theoretical?

**Sanjay:** Product safety processes — tested constantly, embedded in operations. I'm confident there.

Our cyber incident response plan — partially tested. We ran a tabletop exercise eighteen months ago at the corporate IT level but we've never tested the connected vehicle response scenario or a manufacturing OT incident. Those plans exist but I couldn't tell you with confidence that they'd work under real conditions.

Supply chain continuity — not adequately tested. We have business continuity plans for individual plants but we've never stress-tested what a multi-node simultaneous disruption looks like. The semiconductor shortage was the closest we've come and we were not prepared for it.

Treasury hedging — the instruments themselves work, they're tested through normal market operations. But the policy parameters haven't been stress-tested against a scenario as severe as what we saw in 2023-2024. They may not be calibrated for the current volatility regime.

***

**Q18. "Across all the risks you've mentioned — which ones could hit overnight and which would build slowly?"**

**Priya:** Final question — of all the risks we've discussed, which ones do you think could materialize overnight and which ones would you see coming over months?

**Sanjay:** Overnight risks — a cyber attack on vehicle systems, a product safety event requiring immediate recall, a sudden tariff announcement, a battery supplier disruption due to geopolitical event, or a China JV forced restructuring triggered by policy change. These are all scenarios where we could be in crisis within 24 to 48 hours with very little warning.

Slow-build risks — EV adoption falling further behind our projections, Chinese competition gradually taking market share, workforce transition creating persistent labour relations friction, legacy systems degrading over time. These are the risks that don't declare themselves. They erode your position quarter by quarter and the temptation is to keep them lower on the register because nothing dramatic has happened yet. That's actually where I'm most worried about our blind spots — the slow-moving risks that don't trigger the same attention as an acute event but are equally destructive over a three-to-five year horizon.

**Priya:** Sanjay, that's been an incredibly thorough conversation. We'll come back to you with the mapped risk universe and initial scoring within the week. Thank you for your time.

**Sanjay:** Looking forward to it. Thank you Priya.

***
